Apple

  • A raw apple is 86% water and 14% carbohydrates, with negligible content of fat and protein.
  • A reference serving of a raw apple with skin weighing 100 grams provides 52 calories and a moderate content of dietary fiber.
  • All parts of the fruit, including the skin, except for the seeds and the core (from stem to bottom) containing the seeds, are suitable for human consumption.
  • One medium apple — 6.4 ounces or 182 grams offers 2–4% of the RDI for manganese, copper, and the vitamins A, E, B1, B2, and B6.
  • Apples are also a rich source of polyphenols. While nutrition labels don’t list these plant compounds, they’re likely responsible for many of the health benefits.
  • Apples have been linked to a lower risk of heart disease. One reason may be that apples contain soluble fiber — the kind that can help lower your blood cholesterol levels.One of these polyphenols is the flavonoid epicatechin, which may lower blood pressure.
  • Several studies have linked eating apples to a lower risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Apples contain pectin, a type of fiber that acts as a prebiotic. This means it feeds the good bacteria in your gut.
  • Test-tube studies have shown a link between plant compounds in apples and a lower risk of cancer.
  • A large study in more than 68,000 women found that those who ate the most apples had the lowest risk of asthma. Eating about 15% of a large apple per day was linked to a 10% lower risk of this condition.
  • Most research focuses on apple peel and flesh. However, apple juice may have benefits for age-related mental decline.
  • The class of painkillers known as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) can injure the lining of your stomach. A study in test tubes and rats found that freeze-dried apple extract helped protect stomach cells from injury due to NSAIDs.
